As repetition rates scale from 1 Hz to 200 Hz, the Dense Plasma Focus (DPF) transitions from a discrete pulsed system to a quasi-steady-state plasma environment. This transition is characterized by significant shifts in both gas dynamics and breakdown physics.

### 1. Plasma Physics: 1 Hz vs. 200 Hz
At **1 Hz**, the device operates in a "cold" regime where gas fully recovers and electrodes return to thermal equilibrium between shots.
At **200 Hz (5 ms period)**, the physics is dominated by the **"thermal wall"** and cumulative effects:
*   **Heat Load:** The anode heat load scales from negligible levels to extreme fluxes exceeding **4–8 kW/cm²**, necessitating active liquid cooling of the anode and cathode to prevent metal vapor impurity injection (Verma 2013).
*   **Self-Clearing Dynamics:** Lerner (2023) notes that the DPF's axial phase acts as a **supersonic piston** that clears plasma debris from the electrode gap. At 200 Hz, the interval is sufficient for this clearing, but only if the electrode materials (e.g., beryllium or tungsten) can withstand the ion bombardment and heat.
*   **Pre-Conditioning:** High-frequency operation leads to a "steady-state" surface condition on the insulator, reducing shot-to-shot jitter and improving the azimuthal symmetry of the breakdown.

### 2. Residual Ionization: Breakdown and Sheath Formation
Residual ionization (the "memory effect") acts as a natural **pre-ionization mechanism** for high-repetition-rate devices:
*   **Breakdown:** Residual electrons and metastable species from the previous shot lower the breakdown voltage threshold. This reduces **temporal jitter** and promotes a **diffuse (glow-like) breakdown** across the insulator, rather than a filamentary one.
*   **Sheath Formation:** Enhanced pre-ionization improves current sheath symmetry, which is critical for a stable pinch. A symmetric breakdown prevents "spoke" formation, which often degrades yield in single-shot devices.
*   **Time-to-Pinch:** The "time-to-pinch" typically decreases as the repetition rate increases because the avalanche ionization process begins from a non-zero baseline.

### 3. Optimal Fill Pressure Shift
Research on Fast Miniature Plasma Focus (**FMPF**) devices (Verma 2013) identifies a mandatory upward shift in fill pressure for repetitive operation:
*   **Shift Range:** For a 200 J device, the optimal pressure for deuterium shifts from **~3.5 mbar (1 Hz)** to **~5.5 mbar (10 Hz)**. 
*   **Mechanism:** This shift is required to compensate for **decreased gas density** caused by cumulative heating of the gas and electrodes. Higher pressure restores the mass-loading required to match the pinch time to the peak of the current waveform ($t_{pinch} \approx t_{peak}$).
*   **Yield Effect:** When shifted correctly, time-averaged neutron yields can increase by an order of magnitude (e.g., from $10^6$ to $10^7$ n/s) due to the combined effects of higher repetition rate and improved shot-to-shot reproducibility.
